Opportunity Starts with U

The gap between minority students and their educational experiences continues to widen as resources continue to become more rare. Two-thirds of minority students still attend schools that are predominantly minority, most of them located in central cities and funded well below those in neighboring suburban districts. Recent analyses of data prepared for school finance cases in New York has found that "on every tangible measure—from qualified teachers to curriculum offerings—schools serving greater numbers of students of color had significantly fewer resources than schools serving mostly white students."

Why OppU?

The Reasoning Behind it All

Minority students are put at greatest risk by the American tradition of allowing enormous variation in the qualifications of teachers.

25 percent of all new teachers are usually assigned to teach the disadvantaged students in low-income and high-minority schools. Students in poor or predominantly minority schools are less likely to have teachers who are fully qualified or hold higher-level degrees.

Students have less than a 50 percent chance of getting a math or science teacher with a license and a degree in the field.

Roughly 90 percent of the variation in student reading and mathematics scores at grades 3, 6, and 8 was a function of differences in teacher qualifications.

Students of color are often concentrated in schools with fewer resources. Schools with 90 percent or more students of color spend $733 less per student per year than schools with 90 percent or more white students.
